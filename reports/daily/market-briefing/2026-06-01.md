- **Monday post-holiday load recovery + June 1 heat onset**: Commercial/industrial load returns from Memorial Day weekend. - **Solar-exit ramp remains the dominant price mechanism**: The net-load swing from HE18 (41,170 MW) to HE21 (57,965 MW) — a +16,794 MW move in three hours — is the structural driver of all pricing. At HE21, solar is effectively gone (532 MW), wind is still rebuilding from its daytime trough (14,748 MW, recovering but not yet at overnight levels), and gross load remains elevated at 73,869 MW. The math leaves thermal as the marginal resource, hence the spike. Note that the HE21 net load of 57,965 MW (YE) is the highest in this briefing series, surpassing yesterday's HE21 of 54,868 MW by over 3 GW.

- **Daytime wind collapse to 3,817 MW at HE13 is extreme**: The GR_WEST trough of 2,475 MW is less than 25% of its overnight capacity. The system-level wind trough coincides with the solar peak — meaning there is no wind complement when solar is carrying the grid. If any large solar plant trips or cloud cover reduces output HE11-14, the loss of solar cannot be offset by wind, and prices could print well above the $18-20 floor Smartbidder sees. This creates non-trivial RT spike risk in the midday window despite low DA prices.

- **Outage transition from weekend maintenance**: The outage level dropping from 15,421 MW (Sunday avg) to 13,907 MW (Monday avg) represents approximately 1,500 MW of thermal capacity returning to service. This helps the supply picture at peak, but 13,907 MW remains a large offline block — particularly concentrated at HE13-14 (14,375 MW) when thermal is least needed (solar providing).
